🔴 Confidence in Front of Customers — Sev 1 Under Pressure

Created by Dimitris Zoutsos + Leonardo Malaver
The clock is running, the customer is frightened or furious, and there's no fix yet. Diagnosing the incident is the easy part. The hard part is holding the relationship steady while the work is still live — shifting from reactive to reassuring before you have an answer.
✅ What this is

A usable, customer-facing reference for the live-incident conversation — the structured approach, tested example language, two Zendesk macros, and the All Hands demo.

❌ What this is not

A technical runbook for fixing incidents. It runs alongside the Incident Response Process and Response Checklist, which cover how to run the incident itself. The diagnosis is assumed; this is the human side while the fix is still in progress.

🧭 The structured approach

Two things run in parallel

Every Sev 1 is two jobs at once: the incident mechanics — how we work the problem — and the customer conversation — how we hold the relationship. This sprint is about the second, but it only works pinned to the first.

The incident spine — the mechanics

A Sev 1 isn't a straight line — it's a loop. Each stage feeds the next, and what you learn at the close feeds back into how you catch and run the next one.

The conversation framework — H.E.A.R.

📖Where this comes from: H.E.A.R. is our own four-step adaptation for live incidents, riffing on the established HEARD customer-service method (Hear · Empathize · Apologize · Resolve · Diagnose). We reshaped it for the Sev 1 case where there's no fix yet: holding and explaining matter more than apologizing, and the diagnosis is assumed.
H
Hold. Signal ownership before you have a fix. Acknowledge quickly; commit to a specific next update.
E
Explain. Update on a cadence, say what you've ruled out, and lead with "no data loss" only once it's verified.
A
Acknowledge. Meet the emotion before the technical. Frame any customer-side cause as a shared question, not blame.
R
Resolve. Name the cause plainly, admit a wrong first theory, add a preventive step, leave the door open.

What to prioritise, phase by phase

The conversation moves through four phases. In each, the weight goes somewhere specific — and there's one mistake to avoid.

PhaseWhere the weight goesWatch-out
First contactOwnership + next-update timeNo generic holding line
Holding patternCadence + what's ruled outDon't claim "no data loss" unverified
De-escalationImpact first, then technicalDon't get defensive or blame
Resolution & closeCause + prevention + open doorDon't close transactionally
💡The read that sets your tone: is the customer furious or frightened? A furious customer needs you to acknowledge impact first ("a gap in your pipeline isn't acceptable to us either") before any technical detail. A frightened customer needs certainty first ("your data is safe in the stream, nothing is lost") before root cause.

How your tone evolves as the incident matures

The four phases above tell you what to prioritise. As the incident itself progresses — from just-declared through to resolved — they also tell you how your tone should shift. Same incident, different tone depending on where you are:

Verified before reassuring? "No data loss" is the most reassuring thing you can say, and the most damaging if false. Verify first.
Cause possibly customer-side? Frame it as a shared diagnostic question, never an accusation.
Time for a call? When text is going in circles or the customer is highly anxious, offering a call signals confidence, not retreat.
First theory wrong? Say so. Admitting it builds more trust than quietly moving on.

💬 Example language — weak vs strong

The weak line is the instinct under pressure. The strong line is what holds the relationship.

Weak
"We're looking into it and will get back to you."
Strong
"We're on it and treating this as critical. Early signs point to a processing delay, not lost data. I'm confirming now and will update you within 30 minutes, even if it's only to say we're still digging."
Weak
"Still working on it."
Strong
"We've ruled out [X] and are now focused on [Y]. Your events are safe and queued; nothing is being lost. Next update by [time]."
Weak
[no message for two hours]
Strong
"No change to report yet, but I haven't forgotten you — the team is still actively on it. Next check-in in 30 minutes."
Weak
"There's no need to escalate, we're handling it."
Strong
"I understand the impact, and you're right that a repeat isn't acceptable. Here's where we stand. To rule things out, did [X] change on your side?"
Weak
"We follow industry best practice, so this shouldn't be happening."
Strong
"That this has happened more than once is a fair thing to be frustrated about. Let me explain what's different this time and what we're putting in place so it stops."
Weak
"Resolved. Closing this ticket."
Strong
"You're back to normal, no data lost. Cause was [plain-language cause], and we've [preventive step] so it doesn't recur. Reopen the moment anything looks off."
Weak
"It's fixed now."
Strong
"Fixed — and to be straight, our first theory was wrong, which cost us an hour. The real cause was [X]. Here's what we changed so the diagnosis is faster next time."
💡Every phrase is peer-tested: one writes, the other reacts as the customer. If it sounds like an auto-reply spoken aloud, it goes.

When to use this

A Sev 1 is live, there's no resolution yet, and the customer is frightened or furious and pushing for a fix time. You usually know how to fix the pipeline. The harder skill is holding the relationship while you work the problem.

This playbook gives you the customer-facing flow, example language tested on real incidents, two Zendesk macros, and the All Hands demo. It runs alongside the Incident Response Process and Response Checklist, which cover how to run the incident itself.

The flow

The same four steps we already work a Sev 1 by. The difference here is the customer conversation that runs in parallel.

🔁Acknowledge → Act and declare → Customer comms while you work it → Mitigate, resolve, close the loop

1. Acknowledge

Reply inside the SLA window. This does not need a diagnosis. Confirm a human owns it, name what you've understood, and show you're on it. Silence is what makes a frightened customer panic, so the acknowledgement matters more than its content.

If you've already confirmed one thing, share it ("collector is healthy, the drop is downstream"). If not, acknowledge anyway. Don't wait for certainty to make first contact.

2. Act and declare

Declare the incident (/incident in #incidents), open or escalate the Jira card with the correct Priority, and pull in SRE if needed. Do this in parallel with the customer conversation, not after it. Only tickets explicitly escalated through the Jira workflow trigger the urgent SRE alerts, so set Priority correctly or no one is paged.

Declaring early is a customer-facing skill, not just an ops one. The Cargurus incident shows the cost: a 1 hour 7 minute gap between the alert firing (16:48 UTC) and escalation (17:55 UTC), because an Iglu-down alert was treated as "High" rather than an immediate Sev 1. Every minute of internal delay is a minute the customer sits in the dark.

Off-hours, or if SRE is unresponsive, escalate to a Support Leader by Slack then phone (numbers are on Slack profiles). They coordinate with engineering for you. Don't sit waiting.

3. Customer comms while you work it

This is the part the sprint is really about, and it has two jobs: keep the customer feeling looked-after, and shift your tone correctly as the incident moves.

Keep them updated without over-promising. Tell the customer they'll hear from you as things develop, and then actually keep checking in, even when there's no news. "We're still on it, no change yet" is reassuring. Avoid committing to a fix time or a resolution date. If the customer relies on real-time data (personalization, fraud, live decisioning), check in more often and pull in the TAM and CSM. Draft updates at the bottom of the incident Slack Canvas so the message stays consistent across a handover.

When you pull more people in or raise priority, say so plainly: "I'm escalating this internally to get more eyes on it faster." Customers read that as you taking them seriously. De-escalate (slow down, hand back control) only once reality supports it. Too early reads as dismissive.

One read to make early: is the customer furious or frightened? A furious customer needs you to acknowledge the impact first ("a gap in your pipeline isn't acceptable to us either") before any technical detail. A frightened customer needs certainty first ("your data is safe in the stream, nothing is lost") before root cause.

4. Mitigate, resolve, and close the loop

When the IC declares resolution, confirm recovery in plain language and set expectations for the PIR. The postmortem is internal. If the customer wants a written account, produce a separate Customer Incident Summary, never the internal PM. If they ask how you'll prevent or detect this next time, treat it as a trust opportunity and answer concretely (alerting changes, escalation paths, comms preferences).

KB summary (one paragraph for the team KB)

Work a Sev 1 the way we always do, and run the customer conversation in parallel. Acknowledge fast, inside the SLA window and before you have a diagnosis. Act and declare: declare the incident, escalate with the correct Jira Priority, pull in SRE. While you work it, keep the customer updated without promising a fix time, and shift your tone from reactive to reassuring as the incident matures, leading with impact for a furious customer and with data safety for a frightened one. Then mitigate, resolve, and close the loop with a plain-language recovery confirmation and a separate Customer Incident Summary, never the internal PM. We commit to a first response (1hr Standard, 30min Enhanced), never a resolution time, so promise attention and care, not a fix clock.

🔍 How we built this

The work behind the deliverable. Real ticket research, the phase work, and how the pair coordinated.

We worked from three years of escalation and urgent tickets, narrowed to genuine customer-frustration arcs — live incidents where the customer was anxious or angry and the relationship was at stake while the work was unresolved. Coverage runs across every major category: data not landing, loader/warehouse, volume drop/spike, security/CVE, access/SSO, certificate/domain, data quality.

Each thread was read end to end and tagged against four questions: what the customer actually needed to hear vs. the literal question they asked; whether a next-update time was given and kept; how the reactive-to-reassuring pivot went; and whether the close landed honestly or transactionally.

⚠️Findings are generalised. Real names, figures, and internal notes are stripped before any reuse.

The threads that turned tense shared a recognisable shape — the signals a conversation is slipping:

The repeated follow-up. The customer chases two or three times before a substantive reply; by the third message the tone hardens.
"This is business-critical." Stated explicitly because they don't feel it's registered. Having to say it twice escalates frustration fast.
The silence gap. A stretch with no update during active work. The customer fills the silence with worst-case assumptions.
The data-loss fear. The unspoken question under most incidents: "is our data gone?" Addressed early, the thread calms down.
Looping in leadership. The customer CCs their manager or ours — the clearest signal the relationship, not just the ticket, is at risk.
"This keeps happening." A recurring issue carries the weight of every prior occurrence. A fix-only reply reads as dismissive.
The unanswered "why." When a preventable cause surfaces, skipping the why turns relief into renewed frustration.
💡The common thread: discomfort spikes when the customer feels unheard, uninformed, or unsure their data is safe — more than from the incident itself.

Anti-patterns that fracture the relationship

The non-answer. "Looking into it," no owner, no timeline. Reads like an auto-reply.
Premature blame. Questioning the customer's setup before diagnosing. Burns trust in the first reply.
The silence. Going quiet mid-incident. No update is itself an update, and it says "we forgot you."
The defensive pivot. Meeting frustration with "we follow best practice" instead of acknowledging impact.
The transactional close. Marked resolved, no cause named, no door left open.
